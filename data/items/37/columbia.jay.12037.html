The turn European affairs will take because of the death of the King of Spain and the insanity of the King of England is problematical. If peace is made between Turkey and the two empires, Austria and Russia might attack Prussia. Even if the Prince of Wales, who will probably act as Regent, should also be inclined to friendship with Prussia, he will probably not be able to enter a war against Austria and Russia without the consent of parliament. According to an English acquaintance of Jefferson's, the Prince is affable and possesses good common sense, but is almost uneducated and undiscriminating in his choice of companions and amusements. The Duke of York is as much of a profligate, but has less sense. The death of the Speaker of the House of Commons contributes to the disorder in England. France is making progress towards the establishment of a constitution which will place more power in the hands of the people. The vote of the nobles on this question, despite some dissension of some younger members, shows opposition to extending the powers of the people. The Court, which can get no money from either the clergy or the nobles, will probably ally itself with the people. The question of counting votes by persons or estates will have to be decided at the meeting of the States General. Necker's report proposes extending popular power and decreasing that of the King and the ministers. He seems to be able to keep France's financial machinery in motion. The French navy has suffered a grave blow by the death of its Atlantic commander, Bailli de Suffrein, who would have been in charge in case of war with England. The Superior General of the Mathurins, placed in charge of redeeming the Algerian captives by Jefferson, is proceeding quietly with negotiations to avoid suspicion that he is acting as agent for the United States. Jefferson has not yet received an answer concerning the loan from Holland.
