Jefferson hopes that John Jay has recovered from his illness. Information sent from Brest and Le Havre has been delayed by contrary winds. There are many false rumors in Paris. As soon as Jefferson can authenticate a fact, he will send it by the first safe carrier. There has been little activity between Russia and Turkey, and Austria's maneuvers have failed to surprise Belgrade. In cipher, Jefferson writes that the French loan has been filled by subscription, not by cash. It is certain that no money can be obtained in Holland. The hopes of the Dutch patriots are barely alive. The health of the principal French minister is bad, but talk of his retirement is probably not true because of the hold he has on the minds of the King and Queen. No longer in cipher, Jefferson notes that the edict in favor of non-Catholics passed after much discussion. There is violent opposition to the favor shown to American commerce in the edict passed on 29 December 1787. In cipher again, he writes that French officials are alarmed because of the objections of chambers of commerce. The United State can no longer depend on the favor of Luzerne. Without using cipher, he says that the instability of France is such that no merchant cares to speculate on the basis of a law. However, American merchants should not fear that the favorable edict will be discontinued. Jones has gone to Copenhagen to settle the matter of prize money. Jefferson has heard that four American captives have died in Algiers. Since Algiers received a high price from Spain and Naples for the redemption of captives, she will not accept less than five to six hundred chequins a head. Exclusive of the cost of ransom, peace would have cost the United States a million dollars when Lamb was in Algiers. As soon as Congress provides money, Jefferson will begin negotiations for redeeming American captives.
