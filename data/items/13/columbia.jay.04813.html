He and Franklin met with Vergennes on 10 August 1782. The Count with Franklin's concurrence thought that Oswald's commission should be dealt with, while John Jay held that to treat under the description of colonies would be to descend from independence. Later observed to Franklin that it was against French and Spanish interests to acknowledge our independence, while the doctor imputed the Court's conduct to moderation and cited the American ministers' instructions as further reasons for acquiescence. Oswald later agreed to dispatch a courier to London for permission to acknowledge American independence, the answer being that such acknowledgment should be an article in the treaty. Learning that a report sent to Britain by Fitzherbert of Vergennes' acceptance of the commission caused this reply, Jay persuaded Oswald to ask for another giving America equal powers, drafting a letter to this effect which Franklin would not approve. On 9 September learned that Rayneval had gone to England and concluded it was for these reasons: (1) to tell Shelburne that the article recognizing American independence was deemed sufficient by Vergennes; (2) to obtain Britain's agreement to division of the fishery with France exclusively; (3) to impress Shelburne with Spain's wish for exclusive navigation of the Gulf of Mexico and exclusion of the U.S. from the Mississippi while leaving everything north of the Ohio to Britain; (4) to sound out probability of peace on France's terms. Jay persuaded Vaughan to communicate America's sentiments and resolves to Shelburne; the latter returned from England on 27 Sept. with Oswald's new commission. The principal articles were prepared and sent to Britain; Franklin cooperated in obtaining the best possible terms for America. Preliminaries were signed on 30 November. Expected Vergennes' attitude that Jay had "sinned beyond forgiveness." Has esteem and regard for Franklin's behavior; heard of the debates in Congress to censure them but did not know the doctor had prepared the way for them. Wishes Franklin's letter to Moylan might be obtained, or its evidence substantiated.
