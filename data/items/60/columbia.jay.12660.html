Acknowledges receipt of 27 June as well as "the other communications you had the goodness to make at the same time." Washington is sorry to "be assured, of what indeed I had little doubt before, that we have been guilty of violating the treaty in some instances." Washington agrees with John Jay in the opinion that "our affairs are drawing rapidly towards a crisis." Experience has proved that "men will not adopt and carry into execution, measures the best calculated for their own good, without the intervention of a coercive power. I do not conceive we can exist long as a nation without having lodged somewhere a power which will pervade the whole union in as energetic a manner as the authority of the State Governments extend over the several States." To refuse to give such power to Congress appears to Washington to be "the very climax of popular absurdity &madness." It is true that this solution might not be perfect, for "perfection falls not to the share of mortals." Washington also agrees with Jay that if the present state affairs should continue much longer, "that the better kind of people being disgusted with the circumstances, will have their minds prepared for any revolution whatever." Now "even respectable characters speak of a monarchical form of Government without horror. From thinking proceeds speaking, thence to acting is often but a single step." If will be unfortunate if the advocates of despotism find that "we are incapable of governing ourselves, & that systems founded on the basis of equal liberty are merely ideal & fallacious!" Although Washington is retired from the world he is no unconcerned spectator. But he does not feel that he any longer has "some claims to public attention" such as he had during and after the war. Therefore, his opinions would have little weight on the minds of his countrymen.
